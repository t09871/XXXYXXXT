UV / MULTISPECTRAL HUMMINGBIRD RE-ID SYSTEM
===========================================

Concept:
Use UV-reflectance imagery as an additional identity signal for hummingbird Re-ID.

Motivation:
Birds may perceive UV-visible individual markings not present in RGB footage.

Potential architecture:
paired RGB camera
+
paired UV-capable camera

Reason:
UV imagery alone may be difficult for humans to interpret.
Paired synchronized footage could allow:
RGB ↔ UV identity correlation.

Potential workflow:
synchronized feeder footage
-> same timestamp
-> RGB crop
-> UV crop
-> paired identity evidence

Potential future Birdbill module:
multispectral fusion scoring

Possible future scoring:
RGB local features
+
UV reflectance patterns
+
temporal continuity
+
behavioral signatures

Potential hardware:
dual synchronized cameras
shared mount
shared trigger/timestamp
fixed feeder geometry

Open questions:
Do hummingbirds possess stable individual UV-reflectance traits?
How stable are UV patterns across:
angle
molt
lighting
motion blur
wet feathers
gorget flash conditions?

Potential issue:
UV capture likely requires:
full-spectrum camera modification
or
specialized UV-capable hardware.

# Ultrafeeder Project Summary v0.1

# Header: Theory-stage architecture for automated hummingbird feeder sterilization appliance

Date: 2026-06-30

Project Name:
Ultrafeeder

Core Goal:
Develop a proof-of-concept automated hummingbird feeder appliance capable of onboard rinse, steam sterilization, drying, refill, and reassembly cycles using largely off-the-shelf feeder components and hobby-accessible fabrication methods.

Primary Concept Direction:
The project evolved away from “smart feeder” concepts and toward a small autonomous sanitary appliance architecture. Emphasis shifted toward theory-first engineering discussion before visualization or STL generation.

Major Architecture Conclusions:

* Wash/steam chamber should remain stationary.
* Moving the entire wash apparatus or moving hose-connected lids creates excessive mechanical and sanitation complexity.
* Precision wet rails/sliders were rejected due to sugar contamination, steam exposure, and maintenance burden.
* Rotating wet cassettes remain mechanically attractive but introduce sanitation and material concerns due to crevices and repeated steam exposure.
* Current favored direction uses:

  * fixed stainless washpot
  * overhead gantry
  * lightweight movement of only feeder-contact parts
  * stationary plumbing/utilities
  * controlled exhaust and drain systems

Current Proposed Mechanical Architecture:

* Two-position motorized ramekin carousel:

  * one bird-facing feeding position
  * one protected holding/service position
* Overhead gantry system:

  * likely based on off-the-shelf 3D printer gantry hardware
  * stepper-driven
  * potentially G-code controlled
* Stainless fork/hook end effector:

  * fabricated from ~2 mm stainless rod
  * capable of:

    * lifting silicone flower top via stainless lifting eyes
    * lifting ramekin via rolled lip
    * lifting washpot lid
* Fixed stainless washpot:

  * small cylindrical stainless vessel (~1 L or less)
  * integrated drain
  * removable lid
  * rinse/steam/dry chamber
* Steam generator external to chamber:

  * atmospheric low-pressure steam only
  * no pressurized vessel design

Feeder Component Decisions:

* Feeder components should remain largely off-the-shelf.
* No custom molded silicone parts planned for POC.
* Silicone flower tops may be modified with:

  * stainless jump rings
  * stainless lifting eyes
    to allow robotic manipulation.
* Stainless ramekins selected for steam survivability and easy handling via rolled lip geometry.

Material Conclusions:

* PETG considered insufficient for repeated direct steam exposure.
* Polycarbonate printing considered promising for future development.
* Current preferred structure:

  * printed structural parts where appropriate
  * stainless for steam/wet surfaces
  * possible steel frame/enclosure construction
* Steam-contact printed parts should be minimized.

Washpot Input/Output Architecture:
Top/Lid:

* exhaust outlet
* rinse water inlet
* dry air inlet
* movable nectar fill wand
* lid lifting interface

Side:

* low steam inlet
* optional side rinse assist
* temperature probe
* backup vent path

Bottom:

* primary drain
* overflow/leak capture path

Internal:

* ramekin rack
* flower-top rack
* lid parking station

Steam System Conclusions:

* Atmospheric steam only.
* Steam enters low and exits high.
* Controlled exhaust required due to bird-dense deployment environment.
* Exhaust should likely route through:

  * condenser/cooling path
  * finned or baffled cooling chamber
  * controlled vent
* Silicone tubing appears viable for low-pressure steam exhaust if properly routed and drained.

Condensate/Waste Handling:

* All rinse water and condensate should route into removable waste reservoir.
* Standard soda bottle considered attractive removable waste tank solution.
* Waste reservoir:

  * lower shaded compartment
  * replaceable/cleanable
  * non-sterile acceptable
* Hot condensate cooling may require:

  * stainless intermediate trap
  * cooling loop
  * vertical drop section

Dry Cycle Conclusions:
Preferred dry architecture:

* lid remains closed after steam cycle
* filtered dry air forced into chamber
* exhaust exits through same controlled exhaust path
* fan-driven positive airflow preferred
* optional warm-air assist possible

Programming/Controls Direction:

* Strong interest in using:

  * 3D printer control boards
  * G-code motion control
  * printer-style steppers/endstops
* Potentially manageable architecture:

  * carousel stepper
  * gantry motion
  * Z lift
  * water solenoid
  * steam relay
  * fan control
  * temperature monitoring

Key Realization:
The autonomous manipulation system is becoming more complex than the feeder itself. Future development should carefully separate:

* minimum viable sterilization capability
  from
* fully autonomous refill/reassembly ambitions.

Visualization / Design Workflow Notes:

* User explicitly requested theory-first workflow.
* Do not generate sketches/renders unless explicitly requested.
* Prioritize:

  * feasibility
  * failure modes
  * sanitation
  * thermals
  * manufacturability
  * maintenance burden
    before visual exploration.

Project Status:
Theory and architecture stage only. No STL generation yet. Current focus is reducing mechanical/sanitation complexity before committing to CAD architecture.
